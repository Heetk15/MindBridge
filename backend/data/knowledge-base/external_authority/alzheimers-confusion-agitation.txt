# Responding to Agitation and Confusion

Agitation and confusion are common behavioral symptoms of Alzheimer's and other dementias. A person with dementia may become restless, pace, become upset, or experience hallucinations.

## Causes of Agitation
- **Medical reasons**: Pain, infection (such as UTIs), constipation, or medication interactions.
- **Environmental factors**: Overstimulation from noise, clutter, or unfamiliar surroundings.
- **Personal needs**: Hunger, thirst, the need to use the restroom, or lack of sleep.

## How to Respond
1. **Check for pain or illness**: Ensure the person is not suffering from an underlying medical condition.
2. **Create a calm environment**: Reduce noise, clear clutter, and provide soothing music or familiar objects.
3. **Listen to the frustration**: Acknowledge their feelings. Do not try to reason or argue. Say things like, "I understand you're upset" or "You are safe here."
4. **Redirect attention**: Offer a snack, suggest a walk, or involve them in a simple, repetitive task like folding towels.
5. **Keep yourself calm**: Your anxiety can transfer to the person with dementia. Speak in a gentle, reassuring tone.

## When to Call a Doctor
If the agitation is sudden, severe, or poses a danger to the person or others, contact a medical professional immediately. Sudden confusion is often the primary sign of an acute medical issue like a Urinary Tract Infection (UTI).
